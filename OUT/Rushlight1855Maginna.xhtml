Reflected Rays: From the Lamp of Wisdom

Every flight of genius should be upon the wings of reason.

Slander is a poisoned arrow dipped in the venom of envy.

Pleasureis found in the ripples and eddies upon the surface of the stream of time. Peace in the smooth under current.

He who would overtake Time in his flight, must bind on the sandals of diligence.

The jewel of happiness is for sale,locked up in the casket of virtue, and he only can pos sess the jewel who will pur chase the casket.

The web of daily existence should have the strong warp of hope, and for the roof the golden threads of cheerfulness.

The higher the wave of afflictionthat rolls over the spirit, of the good man,the more uncleardistinctand beau tiful, is the white crest of faithupon its summit.

Questions and Answers

Ques.Who among us has the toughest constitution? Ans.The Boardman.

Ques.Why shall we never be out of repair? Ans.We have three Carpenters.

Ques.Upon what shall we depend when we wish to travel by water? Ans.Upon our Briggs and our Ford.

Ques.What source of en couragement have our teachers? Ans.The fact that their Prentice is so diligent.

Ques.What is one of the staple products of this vicinity? Ans. Rice seems to be very abundant

Ques.What unlucky circum stance in regard to our fuel? Ans.When we had Wood , we had no Sawyer , and now that we have a Sawyer , we have no Wood.

Ques.Why are we unsuccessful in Isaak Walton's favorite art? Ans.We have Bates and a Fisher, but the Fish swam away last year?.

Ques.Who is the greatest prophet among us? Ans. Daniels' son.

Ques.Why is it not neces sary for us to attend meeting in stormy weather? Ans.Because we have a Church at home.

Ques.What adjective would we all change from the com parative to the superlative degree? Ans. Wilder to Wildest

Ques.What relic of the feudal ages is among us? Ans. The of Canterbury.

Ques.Why are the inhabitants of Norton a very domestic people? Ans.Because they have sent our Carpenter by Knight to Drown , have moved our Barrows , and intend to annihilate our Sawyer , and all for the benefit of their Holmes.

Ques.If a certain insect is asked, next autumn,Who went out west?What will it reply? Ans. Katy did.

Localand Foreign Intelligence

A band of innocent young creatures, recently entered a room occupied by two young ladies of this Sem- inary , evidently with the intention of cheering them with a lively Serenade.

But strange to relate said young ladies, instead of gratefully listening to their music, or quietly expelling them, rose and put them to death, without a moments warning. Had the musicians belonged to the Hutchinson instead of the Mosquito fam- ily, the affair would have made more noise. Strange! that in the nineteenth century crime is silenced, merely on account of the sufferer's name!

The garden of American Literature

It is a well known fact that we yankees as a nation as a nationare more in- clined to boasting that any other--Well! Why shouldn't we be allowed to commend our wide expanse of coun- try, with its forests primeval, chains of mountains and deep flowing rivers, exalted privileges, and national institutions to the notice of those countries which in spite of themselves, are in constant fear lest sparks from our torch of Liberty blaze forth upon their own soils?

But above all we can justly be proud of our literary field; it has no huge gate with iron bars to prevent the aspiring one from entering the Enclosure but all are welcome-Each if he chooses can cultivate a piece of land, and maintain the whole di- rection of it, either forming it into fancifully arranged flower beds, or intricate paths, labyrinths in fact; and some have been so careless as to allow weeds and brambles to spring up in the otherwise or- namental plot.

Inummerable are the plants which grow in this garden, Ik has a bed of marvellous ones and sometimes Mrs. Parting- ton sends little Ike to help water them, which of- fice he performs with the utmost celerity.

Let us enter this flowery path a moment and glance around. Here is a bed of Ivy formed like a Starr.

Ah! Lizzie Greenwood, you're charming, if you are such a modest little creature-- take care and don't let the Ivy run too far. And now we come suddenly upon a cluster of May Flowers,very beautiful and fragrant are they, though we think they would not be subject to such careful cultivation or flourish so luxuriantly, had not Uncle Tom pitched his Cabinnear them.

And what are these, which toss themselves so complacently in this incense breathing wind? Whole clusters of Fern Leaves to be sure, of all sorts and sizes, some dark and rough, wandering far from the sunlight, they have sprung up in the shade, disagreeable to themselves and others. A widowers weed has caught on one of them, and presents an unsightly appear- ance, and Ruth Hall looks threateningly up from the background. Ah! Fanny, take care that that last mentioned personage doesn't sew thorns in the places you imagined roses and laurels would flourish. Not far from here, Winnie cultivates fine Woodferns to some much more acceptable than Fannys--

Near by Minnie has some fine Myrtles, their dark green leaves being in fine contrast to the flowers which modestly peep forth at beholders.

And now we approach a Greenwood and dearly do we love to throw ourselves at the foot of some majestic tree, and watch Grace as she rides past on horse- back or take up her letters and read of events both amusing and interesting. Lately she has adopted a Little Pilgrim who care- fully preserves the trees from decay.

There has been a For rester who delighted to watch over three young plants and often cultivated rich and rare ones; long she resided in that land, Where the rose never blooms on fair womans wan cheek. But there's beautiful light in her eye. And the smile that she wears is so loving and meek, None can doubt that it comes down from the sky.

And to that sky she has now gone, and as the evening shades gather round her grave, a little birdling mourn- fully sings her requiem.
